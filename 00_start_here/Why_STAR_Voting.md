# Why STAR Voting

### Slide 1 — Why STAR Voting?

- Score Then Automatic Runoff
- A better ballot, a fairer winner, a count anyone can verify

### Slide 2 — The Problem Isn't Just Spoilers

- Choose-One Plurality forces your whole opinion into a single name
- That single act causes: vote splitting, the spoiler effect, lesser-evil voting
- Winners can take office without ever beating anyone in a head-to-head
- Root problem: the ballot won't let voters say what they think — so results don't reflect what voters want

> Speaker note: Lead with the deeper failure (the ballot can't express the electorate), not just "spoilers," so RCV-IRV folks can't say "we fix that too."

### Slide 3 — What STAR Voting Is

- Score every candidate 0 to 5 by how much you support them
- **Scoring round:** the two highest-scored candidates become finalists
- **Automatic runoff:** every ballot counts as a full vote for whichever finalist it scored higher
- Two simple steps: degree of support, then a clear head-to-head finish

### Slide 4 — You Never Have to Betray Your Favorite

- Supporting a new candidate you love never costs a candidate you also like
- Score both high — no forced ranking, no "wasted" vote
- Express how much you support each candidate, not just a single pick
- Honesty is your best ballot

### Slide 5 — Every Winner Earns a Majority

- The automatic runoff guarantees the winner beats the runner-up head-to-head
- Among all voters who expressed a preference between the finalists, the winner has majority support
- Expressiveness of scoring plus the legitimacy of a final round
- No more 10%-plurality winners

> Speaker note: This is the differentiator against anyone who claims majoritarianism. STAR is the synthesis of expressive scoring and a real majority finish.

### Slide 6 — Simple to Vote

- It's the familiar five-star rating — movies, rideshares, now elections
- Equal scores allowed: rate two candidates the same if you feel the same
- Low ballot-error rates; no confusing forced strict order
- No exhausted (discarded) ballots in the runoff

### Slide 7 — Easy to Count and Verify

- STAR is precinct-summable: each precinct reports its score totals and its For/Equal/Against pairwise matrix
- Add the precincts together — no central tabulation required
- Any precinct's numbers can be checked and added independently
- Transparent, auditable, fast

### Slide 8 — Better Incentives for Candidates

- To win, you want to be the high score of your opponents' supporters too
- Rewards broad appeal over base-energizing and mudslinging
- Tends toward broadly supported, consensus winners
- Healthier campaigns by design

### Slide 9 — Equal Vote

- Every voter has the identical 0–5 range for every candidate
- Identical maximum influence in both the scoring round and the runoff
- Passes the Test of Balance: any support a ballot can express can be met by an equal and opposite opposition
- Equal weight, structurally

### Slide 10 — What STAR Does (and Doesn't) Claim

- **Does:** end favorite betrayal, guarantee a majority finish, stay easy to count
- **Dramatically reduces** vote splitting (no method eliminates all strategy)
- **Usually** elects the Condorcet winner — very high rate, not a guarantee
- **Resistant** to strategy, not strategy-proof (no method can be)
- Honesty about limits is why you can trust the rest

> Speaker note: This slide builds credibility. Audiences trust a reformer who states the boundary lines.

### Slide 11 — What You Can Do

- Try a STAR ballot at BetterVoting.com
- Bring STAR to your next club, org, or local election
- Learn more / get involved with the Equal Vote Coalition

# Part 2 — Tiered Debate-Prep Talking Points

Each argument: the claim (in precise, defensible wording), the rebuttal it tends to draw, and your response.

## Tier 1 — Unshakeable (lead with these)

**1. Expressiveness — no favorite betrayal**

- **Claim:** Score every candidate 0–5 by how much you support them; backing a new favorite never costs a candidate you also like.
- **Rebuttal:** "Ranked ballots also let you express more than one choice."
- **Response:** Ranked ballots force a strict order and can't express equal support or degree of support. STAR lets you score two candidates the same (Equal Preference) and say how much more you support one over another. And RCV-IRV's later rounds can still punish your favorite via center-squeeze — STAR's scoring round can't.

**2. Automatic runoff → a guaranteed majority finish**

- **Claim:** The two highest-scored candidates advance; every ballot then counts as a full vote for whichever finalist it scored higher, so the winner has majority support in that head-to-head.
- **Rebuttal:** "Score voting just elects the highest average — a small, enthusiastic minority can win."
- **Response:** That's precisely why STAR has the runoff. Pure score is vulnerable to that; the automatic runoff guarantees the winner beats the runner-up among everyone who expressed a preference between them. STAR is the synthesis, not pure score.

**3. Summability, transparency, auditability**

- **Claim:** STAR is precinct-summable — report each precinct's score totals and its For/Equal/Against pairwise matrix and add them up. No central tabulation needed.
- **Rebuttal:** "Modern RCV-IRV is auditable too; the software handles it."
- **Response:** RCV-IRV is not additively summable. Eliminations are sequential and depend on the full ballot set, so precincts can't be tallied independently and then combined — you need the complete cast-vote record centrally. STAR's two summable data structures let any precinct's totals be verified and added on their own. This is a structural property of the method, not a software feature.

**4. Usability**

- **Claim:** 0–5 scoring is the familiar five-star rating; equal scores are allowed; ballot-error rates are low and there are no exhausted ballots in the runoff.
- **Rebuttal:** "RCV-IRV is already used widely; voters handle it fine."
- **Response:** Ranked ballots have measurably higher overvote/error rates and produce exhausted (discarded) ballots when a voter doesn't rank a finalist. STAR ballots aren't exhausted in the runoff the same way, and allowing equal scores removes the cognitive load of a forced strict ordering.

## Tier 2 — Needs Precise Framing (true with the right words)

**5. Reduces vote splitting / the spoiler effect**

- **Claim:** STAR dramatically reduces vote splitting and the spoiler effect.
- **Rebuttal:** "You admit it doesn't eliminate it — so it's not solved."
- **Response:** No deterministic method with three or more candidates eliminates strategic incentives entirely (Gibbard). STAR removes the ordinary spoiler dynamic — running a similar candidate doesn't split support, because voters can score both highly — and shrinks the residual to a narrow, hard-to-coordinate edge case. "Dramatically reduces, doesn't claim to eliminate" is honest and still winning.

**6. Condorcet compliance**

- **Claim:** STAR elects the Condorcet winner in the large majority of realistic elections.
- **Rebuttal:** "STAR is not Condorcet-compliant — it can fail to elect the candidate who beats all others head-to-head."
- **Response:** Correct, and we don't claim otherwise. STAR isn't Condorcet-compliant, but simulations put its Condorcet-efficiency very high; when it diverges, the winner is someone with very high overall support who lost one pairing narrowly. And Condorcet methods buy compliance by sacrificing other properties — the Condorcet winner can be a weakly supported compromise nobody is enthusiastic about. It's a tradeoff, not a clean loss.

**7. Strategy resistance**

- **Claim:** A voter's best play is usually sincere or near-sincere scoring.
- **Rebuttal:** "STAR is manipulable — min/max (bullet) scoring is a strategy."
- **Response:** Every method is manipulable — Gibbard–Satterthwaite for ranked methods, Gibbard's 1978 result for cardinal ones. (Note: this is Gibbard, not Arrow — Arrow is about consistently aggregating rankings, a different result.) The real question is how often strategy pays and how risky it is. Under STAR, min/maxing can backfire: collapsing your scores can hand the runoff to a candidate you'd have preferred to influence, and it forfeits your say in choosing the finalists. Resistant, not proof — and more resistant than Choose-One or RCV-IRV on realistic electorates.

**8. Consensus winners**

- **Claim:** STAR rewards broad appeal and tends to elect consensus winners.
- **Rebuttal:** "'Consensus' is vague — define it."
- **Response:** Operationalize it: STAR picks a runoff-moderated maximizer of total expressed support. It avoids both the plurality winner (narrow base, Choose-One) and the pure-average winner (manipulable, pure score). The runoff then ensures that consensus winner also wins a majority head-to-head against the strongest rival.

## Tier 3 — Contestable / Values-Based (win on framing, know the tradeoff)

**9. Later-no-harm — feature, not bug** *(the central RCV-IRV attack)*

- **Claim:** Later-no-harm is not a property we should want; honestly supporting a compromise candidate should be able to help them win.
- **Rebuttal:** "Under STAR, giving any points to your second choice can cause your first choice to lose. RCV-IRV guarantees that ranking later choices never hurts your favorite."
- **Response:** Woodall proved later-no-harm is provably incompatible with other desirable criteria — it isn't free. The price RCV-IRV pays for it is center-squeeze: a broadly liked candidate can be eliminated early precisely because few rank them first, handing the win to a more polarizing candidate the majority actually opposed. STAR's design says if enough voters genuinely support a compromise, that support should count. Reframe it: not "your vote hurt your favorite," but "your honest support helped elect someone a majority preferred."

**10. Equality / Test of Balance**

- **Claim:** STAR gives every voter equal weight and passes the Test of Balance — any support a ballot can express can be exactly countered by an equal and opposite opposition.
- **Rebuttal:** "Score/STAR violates one-person-one-vote — some voters hand out more total points."
- **Response:** Total points are irrelevant to influence: every voter has the identical 0–5 range for every candidate and identical maximum leverage in both the scoring round and the runoff. The Test of Balance is the precise version of equal weight. Caution: don't claim other methods violate the constitutional One-Person-One-Vote doctrine. Reynolds v. Sims-line OPOV governs apportionment and district population equality, not ballot expressiveness. The equal-weight argument is structural and strong; the constitutional-violation claim is a stretch a lawyer will call.

**11. Reduces polarization / negative campaigning**

- **Claim:** Because candidates want to be the high score of rivals' supporters, STAR rewards broad outreach over base-energizing attacks.
- **Rebuttal:** "That's speculative — where's the evidence?"
- **Response:** It's an incentive-structure argument, well-grounded in theory and consistent with limited real-world use, but the large-scale empirical record is thin because adoption is recent. Present it as a predicted incentive effect, not a proven outcome. Conceding that boundary protects your credibility on everything else.

## Quick-reference: what to lead with, by audience

- **General public / first contact:** Tier 1 #1 (no favorite betrayal) + #2 (majority finish). Emotional and unshakeable.
- **Election administrators / officials:** Tier 1 #3 (summability) + #4 (usability). Concrete and operational.
- **RCV-IRV advocates:** open on Tier 1 #2 and #3 (take majoritarianism and transparency); be ready for Tier 3 #9 (later-no-harm) and reframe it as center-squeeze.
- **Academics / skeptics:** Tier 2 throughout, stated with the precise caveats. Your willingness to say "resistant, not proof" and "not Condorcet-compliant" is what earns the room.
